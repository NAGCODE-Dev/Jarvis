# Mobility, Flexibility, Tendons, Ligaments, and Injury Prevention

## Executive Summary

Mobility is task-specific usable range of motion under control. Flexibility is passive range. Injury prevention is not one drill or warm-up; it is load management, tissue capacity development, technique, exposure progression, and sport-specific preparation.

## Complete Explanation

### Fundamental Concepts

- Flexibility: passive ROM.
- Mobility: active ROM with positional control.
- Tendons transmit force and adapt slowly.
- Ligaments stabilize joints and have slower metabolic turnover than muscle.

### Intermediate Concepts

- Strength through range often improves practical mobility better than passive stretching alone.
- Stretching can improve ROM; whether it improves performance depends on dose, timing, and task.
- Tendon health benefits from progressive loading, isometrics in some pain contexts, and patience.
- Injury risk is multifactorial and weakly predicted by single-screen tests.

### Advanced Concepts

- The main prevention lever is appropriate chronic load progression.
- Excess fatigue, abrupt spikes, poor preparation, and returning too fast after pain episodes are common failure points.
- Tissue tolerance varies by sport: heavy slow resistance, plyometric stiffness work, and skill exposure all have roles.

## Best Practices

- Warm up by raising temperature, rehearsing positions, and priming task-specific force.
- Build end-range strength if the sport requires it.
- Progress plyometrics and high-strain tendon work carefully.

## Common Errors

- Treating mobility as endless stretching.
- Blaming every pain issue on "tightness."
- Returning to prior volume too quickly after symptoms calm down.

## Divergences

- Static stretching before explosive tasks: evidence suggests large doses may reduce acute force or power; small or targeted doses may be acceptable when ROM access is the limiting factor.
- Screening tools: useful for discussion and observation, weak as deterministic injury predictors.
