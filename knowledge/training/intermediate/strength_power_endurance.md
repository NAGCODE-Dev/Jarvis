# Strength, Power, and Endurance

## Executive Summary

Strength, power, muscular endurance, and cardiovascular endurance overlap but are not interchangeable. Programming should treat them as related but distinct adaptations with different loading, velocity, rest, and specificity requirements.

## Complete Explanation

### Maximal Strength

- Depends heavily on neural adaptation, task skill, and high-force expression.
- Heavier loads have strong specificity for 1RM performance.
- Long rest periods usually improve quality and repeatability.

### Power

- Power is force x velocity.
- It requires both enough force capacity and practice producing force quickly.
- Olympic lifts, jumps, throws, and dynamic effort methods can all contribute depending on context.

### Muscular Endurance

- Local ability to sustain repeated contractions.
- Improved by repeated task-specific work, pacing, and local fatigue tolerance.

### Cardiovascular Endurance

- Driven by central and peripheral aerobic adaptation.
- Must be trained with modality-specific exposure when performance matters.

## Best Practices

- Keep competition lifts central for strength athletes.
- Use ballistic or explosive work before fatigue-heavy assistance.
- Use conditioning that matches the target sport and joints you can recover from.

## Common Errors

- Training everything at once with no priority.
- Treating barbell speed loss and cardiovascular fatigue as the same thing.
- Using power exercises under excessive fatigue and calling it speed work.

## Practical Applications

- Powerlifting peak: high specificity, moderate variation, controlled fatigue.
- Weightlifting power phase: technical lifts plus pulls, squats, jumps, and readiness management.
- CrossFit conditioning block: mixed-modal intervals, pacing practice, sport skill retention.
