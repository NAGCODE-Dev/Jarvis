# Hypertrophy

## Executive Summary

Muscle hypertrophy is best explained by sufficient tension distributed across enough hard sets over time with progressive exposure and adequate recovery. Current evidence supports a range of loads, a broad rep range, and the importance of weekly volume and proximity to failure.

## Complete Explanation

### Fundamental Concepts

- Hypertrophy is the enlargement of muscle fibers and associated tissue.
- Mechanical tension is the main training driver.
- Weekly volume is a major controllable variable.
- Load matters less than once believed if sets are taken sufficiently hard.

### Intermediate Concepts

- Sets close to failure recruit high-threshold motor units, especially with lighter loads.
- Longer muscle lengths often appear favorable for hypertrophy when exercises can load those positions well.
- Exercise selection affects regional hypertrophy.
- Stable exercises usually allow better local output for hypertrophy than highly unstable ones.

### Advanced Concepts

- There is likely a recoverable dose-response curve rather than a simple "more is always better" rule.
- Frequency mainly acts as a way to distribute quality volume.
- Volume landmarks are practical heuristics, but individual response varies widely.

## Best Practices

- Use 2-3 weekly exposures per muscle group when possible.
- Start with moderate recoverable volume and progress only if performance, soreness, and motivation remain acceptable.
- Use compounds plus accessories.
- Keep most sets around 0-3 RIR for efficient hypertrophy work.

## Common Errors

- Chasing novelty instead of adding high-quality hard sets.
- Mistaking pump for growth.
- Using only low reps or only very high reps.
- Letting technique degrade so much that target tissues lose tension.

## State of the Art

- Volume matters.
- Load can vary widely when effort is sufficient.
- Failure is not mandatory on every set, but being too far from failure reduces stimulus.
- Exercise choice and lengthened-position loading are active research areas.

## Evidence Notes

- High vs low load: hypertrophy can be similar when sets are hard enough; strength tends to favor heavier training specificity.
- Frequency: generally useful as a volume distribution tool.
- Failure vs non-failure: similar outcomes are possible when total hard work is equated.
