# Exercise Physiology and Energy Systems

## Executive Summary

Training adaptations are specific to the stress applied. Acute performance depends on substrate availability, neural drive, and fatigue resistance. Chronic adaptation depends on repeated exposure plus recovery.

## Complete Explanation

### Fundamental Concepts

- ATP-PCr system dominates short maximal efforts.
- Glycolytic contribution rises in repeated high-intensity work lasting roughly tens of seconds to a few minutes.
- Oxidative metabolism supports longer efforts and recovery between bouts.
- Muscle adaptation includes neural, metabolic, structural, and connective tissue changes.

### Intermediate Concepts

- Strength and power rely heavily on neural coordination and high-force motor unit recruitment.
- Hypertrophy relies on sufficient mechanical tension, enough effective work, and recoverable volume.
- Conditioning depends on central adaptations like stroke volume and peripheral adaptations like mitochondrial and capillary changes.
- Fatigue is task-specific: phosphagen depletion, metabolite accumulation, neural inhibition, thermal stress, and glycogen depletion all matter in different tasks.

### Advanced Concepts

- CrossFit-style mixed-modal work is not explained by one energy system; pacing strategy and movement economy become decisive.
- Interference can occur when endurance work is badly placed relative to strength/hypertrophy work, especially if volume is high and modality overlaps.
- Recovery kinetics matter for set design: short rests increase metabolic stress but can reduce force output; long rests preserve performance.

## Best Practices

- Match intervals to the target adaptation.
- Separate hard endurance and lower-body strength sessions when possible.
- Use rest intervals according to goal rather than habit.
- Interpret heart rate, pace, power, and bar speed as context-dependent tools, not universal truth.

## Common Errors

- Calling every hard workout "anaerobic."
- Assuming "fat burning zone" is the main driver of body composition.
- Using the same conditioning prescription for all sports and lifters.
